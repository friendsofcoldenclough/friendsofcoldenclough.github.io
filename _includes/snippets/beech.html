The Common Beech (Fagus sylvatica) is only considered truly native to southeast England and southeast Wales. It grows in woods or as single trees, usually on drier, free-draining soils, such as chalk, limestone and light loams.

Beech woodland is shady and characterised by a dense carpet of fallen leaves and mast husks which prevent most woodland plants from growing. Only specialist shade-tolerant plants can survive beneath a beech canopy.

Thinning the beech saplings in areas of woodland will allow native species such as oak, silver birch, downy birch and rowan to thrive where beech would otherwise take over.
